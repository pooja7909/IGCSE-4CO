Past Papers & Practice

A guide to every past series of the 4CP0 specification, what each paper tests, and a six-week plan for working through them properly.

📋 The two papers — at a glance

First, know what you're walking into. Both papers are 50% of your final grade.

Paper 1 — Principles of Computer Science

4CP0/01 · 2 hours · 80 marks · 50%
  • Format: written exam (pen and paper, no computer)
  • Coverage: All 6 topics (Topic 2 = programming theory; the practical of Topic 2 is on Paper 2)
  • Question types: short-answer (1–3 marks), structured (4–8 marks), extended-response (6–10 marks)
  • Common high-mark areas: trace tables, binary arithmetic, CPU cycles, network protocols, ethical evaluation
  • Calculators: NOT allowed

Paper 2 — Application of Computational Thinking

4CP0/02 · 3 hours · 80 marks · 50%
  • Format: on-screen practical exam (Python 3)
  • Coverage: Primarily Topic 2 (programming) — but draws on Topic 1 (algorithms) and Topic 3 (data/encryption)
  • Question types: 4–8 progressively harder tasks; you write code from scratch and save it
  • Common high-mark areas: validation loops, file I/O, 2D lists, functions with parameters & return, ord/chr ciphers
  • Resources: blank Python IDE + on-screen reference sheet only
🎯 Mark-scheme insight: Across both papers, around 40% of marks come from techniques you can drill once and re-use — trace tables, binary conversions, the fetch-decode-execute steps, the line.strip().split(",") pattern, and validation loops. Master these patterns and you start every paper with a 30+ mark cushion.

🔍 Where to find the actual papers

Ask your teacher first — schools usually have a folder of past papers and mark schemes. If you're studying independently:

  • Your school's exam-revision portal — most schools have a shared drive with Edexcel materials.
  • Pearson's qualification pages — the official source for specifications, specimen materials, and approved sample assessments.
  • Third-party revision sites — many host archived past papers and mark schemes for IGCSE Computer Science Edexcel.

Always check you have the right paper code (4CP0/01 or 4CP0/02) — the older 4CP0 spec uses a similar number but different content.

📆 The six-week practice plan

If you have six weeks until your exam, this is the optimal order to attack past papers. Each week mixes new content with revisited mistakes.

Week Focus Action
Week 1 Specimen + 2019 — gentle start Sit Paper 1 (specimen) untimed. Mark strictly. Identify your weakest topic. Spend the rest of the week revising that one topic on this hub.
Week 2 2021 Nov + 2022 June Sit each Paper 1 in 2 hours timed. Sit each Paper 2 in 3h timed (use the Code Lab for the practical). Mark and log every lost mark with a reason.
Week 3 Topic deep-dives Re-do the weakest 2 topics from your error log. Hit every quiz question on those topics until you score 90%+.
Week 4 2023 June + 2024 June Sit both papers timed. By now your error log should show patterns — vocabulary, trace tables, or specific question types. Pin them down.
Week 5 Targeted Paper-2 practice Spend the week in the Python Code Lab. Aim to solve all 10 tasks unaided. Time yourself on 2–3 of them — Paper 2 is brutal on time.
Week 6 Mock + review Sit one full mock under exam conditions (any year you haven't redone). Mark immediately. The last 3 days: revisit ONLY topics you got wrong. No new material.
🧠 The error-log habit: Every wrong answer goes in a notebook with three columns: (1) the question, (2) your mistake, (3) the correct approach in one sentence. Review the notebook once a week. This single habit raises grades more than any extra hour of new revision.

⏱ Time management in the actual exam

Paper 1 — 80 marks in 120 minutes means roughly 1.5 minutes per mark. So a 4-mark question gets ~6 minutes, a 10-mark extended response gets ~15 minutes.

  • Don't get stuck. If a question takes 50% longer than its mark allowance, skip it and come back.
  • Leave 5 minutes at the end to re-check binary arithmetic and trace tables (the highest-error questions).
  • For extended responses, plan with a 3-bullet outline FIRST — examiners give marks for structure.

Paper 2 — 80 marks in 180 minutes means about 2.25 minutes per mark, more generous BUT every task requires typing AND testing AND saving.

  • READ the whole paper for 5 minutes BEFORE coding. The later tasks often build on earlier ones.
  • Save constantly. Every task saves to its own file with a specific filename — losing that = losing all the marks.
  • Comment your code as you write it. If something doesn't work, examiners can still award marks for the logic visible in your comments.
  • Don't aim for one perfect task. Aim for 6 partially-working tasks rather than 1 perfect one.
